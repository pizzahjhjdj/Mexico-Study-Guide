‚ö° The Executive Branch

üîë Key Concept: The Mexican presidency is historically very powerful ‚Äî under PRI rule, the president essentially controlled all levels of government. Since democratization, power has become more checked, but the executive remains dominant.
Presidential Requirements
  • Native-born Mexican of native-born parents
  • At least 35 years old
  • No religious leaders (priests/ministers barred)
  • Directly elected ‚Äî First-Past-the-Post (FPTP/plurality)
  • No runoff ‚Äî winner just needs most votes (Nieto won with ~1/3)
  • Serves ONE 6-year term only ‚Äî Sexenio
Formal Powers of the President
  • Initiates ~90% of all legislation
  • Extensive appointment powers (cabinet, etc.)
  • Make policy by decree (executive order equivalent)
  • Veto legislation
  • Control foreign policy
  • Grant pardons
  • Can have Senate remove state governors
Informal Powers (Especially under PRI era)

Under PRI dominance, the president managed the patronage system ‚Äî naming winning candidates for governors, senators, deputies, and local officials. Until the 1990s, the incumbent president chose the next presidential candidate through a practice called dedazo ("finger-pointing"). Between 1929‚Äì1992, all presidential legislation was approved by Congress without challenge.

Section 04

üó≥ Electoral Systems & Reforms

Single-Member Districts (SMD / FPTP)
  • Each district elects ONE representative
  • Winner = most votes (no majority needed)
  • Promotes 2-party dominant systems
  • Ensures geographic representation
  • Mexico uses this for 300 of 500 House seats
Proportional Representation (PR)
  • Seats allocated by percentage of total votes
  • Results in MORE political parties
  • Increases women & minority candidate representation
  • Prevents minority rule
  • Mexico uses for 200 of 500 House seats + 32 Senate seats

Section 05

‚öñ The Judiciary

Key Point: The judiciary is officially the weakest branch of government. While it has formal powers of judicial review, historically it does NOT rule against government action or policy.
Supreme Court Structure
  • 11 justices (reduced from 26 in 1994)
  • Nominated by president, approved by Senate
  • Supposed to serve 15-year terms
  • 8 of 11 must agree to strike down a law (high bar)
  • Has power of judicial review ‚Äî rarely exercises it against gov't
Why the Judiciary is Weak
  • Justices historically resigned after elections so presidents could handpick replacements
  • History of not challenging executive or legislative action
  • No tradition of judicial independence under PRI era
  • Immense current pressure for reform
  • Unlike US, judiciary has NOT been a meaningful check on power

üé≠ Political Parties

Mexico has a multiparty system with three historically dominant parties and a newer insurgent party. Unlike Britain (2-party dominant) or Russia (1-party dominant), Mexico features genuinely competitive elections across a left-right spectrum.

PRI ‚Äî Institutional Revolutionary Party
CENTER / HISTORICALLY DOMINANT
  • Founded as coalition of elites; corporatist structure
  • Three-legged stool: laborers, peasants, bureaucrats
  • Patron-client system (clientelism)
  • Anticlerical ‚Äî limited church power
  • Values: land reform, peasant empowerment
  • Ruled 1929‚Äì2000 without interruption
  • Regained power 2012‚Äì2018 (Pe√±a Nieto)
  • Typical voter: rural, older, less educated, poorer
PAN ‚Äî National Action Party
CENTER-RIGHT
  • Created to represent business interests
  • Advocates regional autonomy & less gov't intervention
  • Pro-Catholic Church (good rapport)
  • Strength in Northern Mexico
  • Won presidency 2000 (Fox) and 2006 (Calder√≥n)
  • Little impact until 1990s
  • Typical voter: urban, middle class, educated, religious
PRD ‚Äî Democratic Revolutionary Party
CENTER-LEFT
  • Split off from PRI ‚Äî wanted more reform & social justice
  • Suffers from internal division; never won presidency
  • Precursor to MORENA's rise
  • Typical voter: young, politically active, central states, urban
MORENA ‚Äî National Regeneration Movement
LEFT-WING ‚Äî CURRENTLY DOMINANT
  • Founded 2011, registered as party 2014
  • Democratic left: diversity, human rights, environment
  • Opponent of neoliberal economic policies since 1980s
  • Won presidency 2018 (AMLO, 53% of vote) + majority in both chambers
  • Focuses on reducing pay gap, not raising taxes on wealthy
  • Currently in power: President Sheinbaum (2024)
Section 07

ü§ù Civil Society & Media

Civil Society
  • Defined as collective action of citizens based on shared interests & values
  • PRI practiced state corporatism ‚Äî divided interest groups into labor, peasants, middle class
  • Dominant labor union: CTM (Confederation of Mexican Workers) ‚Äî created by PRI
  • 85% of Mexicans belong to NO civil organization ‚Äî reflects authoritarian legacy
  • Rise of vigilante groups against drug-related violence
  • PRI's downfall started with discontented businessmen excluded from system
Media
  • Little independence under PRI ‚Äî government-run, part of patron-client rewards
  • More independence in 1980s with satellite TV access
  • Two main TV networks historically supported PRI
  • Since 2000: more vibrant, critical media environment
  • Major problem: Cartels pressure and murder investigative journalists ‚Äî chilling effect on press freedom
Corporatism vs. Pluralism: Under the PRI's corporatist system, the state organized and controlled interest groups (labor, peasants, middle class) as part of the governing party structure. This is very different from pluralist democracies where independent groups compete freely to influence government.

üìñ Essential Key Terms

Unit 2 Vocabulary You Must Know

SEXENIO

Mexico's single 6-year presidential term; no re-election allowed.

DEDAZO

Outgoing PRI president's practice of hand-picking his successor.

CLIENTELISM

Patron-client system: powerful patron offers resources (jobs, land) in exchange for political support (votes, labor).

CORPORATISM

State organizes society into official groups (labor, peasants, business) integrated into the governing party structure.

PRI / PAN / PRD / MORENA

Mexico's four main political parties ‚Äî dominant, right, left, and new left respectively.

PROPORTIONAL REPRESENTATION

Electoral system where seats are allocated based on each party's share of the vote.

SMD (Single-Member District)

Each geographic district elects one rep; winner-take-all (FPTP).

PARASTATAL SECTOR

Semi-autonomous government agencies producing goods/services (e.g. PEMEX, the national oil company).

MIXED ELECTORAL SYSTEM

Mexico uses BOTH SMD (300 seats) and PR (200 seats) for the Chamber of Deputies.

FPTP (First Past the Post)

Electoral system where the candidate with the most votes wins ‚Äî no majority required; no runoff.

IFE (Federal Electoral Institute)

Independent body created 1996 to oversee and regulate elections; key step in democratization.

CTM

Confederation of Mexican Workers ‚Äî dominant labor union created by and historically aligned with PRI.
